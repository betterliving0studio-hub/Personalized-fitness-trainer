Master the Perfect Push-Up

Build strength, power, and endurance with the ultimate bodyweight exercise

Overview

Push-ups are one of the most effective and accessible bodyweight exercises that have stood the test of time. They target your chest, shoulders, triceps, and core muscles simultaneously, making them a complete upper-body workout. Perfect for both beginners and advanced athletes, push-ups improve upper-body strength, posture, stability, and functional fitness. Whether you're training at home, at the gym, or outdoors, push-ups require nothing but your own body weight and determination. They can be modified to suit any fitness level and progressively challenged as you get stronger.

How to Do Push-Ups

Step 1: Starting Position

Begin in a high plank position with your hands slightly wider than shoulder-width apart. Your body should form a straight line from your head to your heels. Keep your core engaged, shoulders over your wrists, and look slightly ahead, not down. Press your palms firmly into the ground and spread your fingers wide for better stability.

Step 2: Lower Down

Slowly bend your elbows and lower your body in a controlled manner until your chest nearly touches the floor. Keep your core tight, back flat, and elbows at a 45-degree angle to your body. Inhale as you descend. Make sure your hips don't sag and your shoulders stay engaged throughout the movement.

Step 3: Push Up

Exhale as you push back up to the starting position by extending your arms fully. Press through your palms and engage your chest muscles. Avoid locking your elbows at the top of the movement. Control the movement and maintain a rigid body position. Repeat for your desired number of repetitions.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Sagging or Rising Hips

Keep your body in a straight line. Engage your core throughout the entire movement to maintain proper alignment.

Flaring Elbows Outward

Keep elbows at 45 degrees to your body. This protects your shoulders and ensures proper muscle engagement.

Not Engaging Core

Tighten your core before starting. A strong core stabilizes your entire body and prevents injury.

Incomplete Range of Motion

Lower your chest close to the floor and fully extend your arms. Full range of motion maximizes muscle engagement.
